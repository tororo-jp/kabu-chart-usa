About This Tool

All S&P 500 constituents (~503 stocks) are automatically scanned every trading day after market close (ET), and stocks with strong technical setups are displayed ranked by score.

This tool is for informational purposes only. Scores and probabilities are mechanical calculations and do not guarantee future price movements. All investment decisions are at your own risk.

Key Features

  • 30+ chart pattern detection (Inverse H&S, Double Bottom, VCP, Perfect Order, etc.)
  • 20+ technical indicators (RSI, MACD, ADX, Ichimoku, OBV, etc.)
  • Composite scoring across 5 categories (0–100 points)
  • Algorithmic calculation of win probability, target price, stop-loss, and RR ratio
  • Interactive TradingView-style candlestick charts
  • Automated backtest of historical signals (weekly update)
  • Favorites saved in browser localStorage

Data Flow

1
Price FetchHistorical OHLCV data for the past 400 trading days fetched via Yahoo Finance (yfinance). Batch download of 50 stocks at a time.
2
Indicator CalculationSMA, EMA, RSI, MACD, ADX, ATR, OBV, Ichimoku, and 20+ other indicators computed with pandas.
3
Pattern Detectionscipy.signal and custom logic detect 30+ chart patterns, each assigned a confidence score (0–1.0).
4
ScoringTrend, Pattern, Momentum, Volume, and RS evaluated across 5 categories on a 100-point scale.
5
PublishResults written to results.json → committed to GitHub → auto-deployed to GitHub Pages → viewable in browser.

Five categories are combined for a 100-point total. Higher score = more technical indicators simultaneously favorable.

⑤ RS Score (max 5 pts)

The 12-month price change of all stocks scanned that day is ranked. Top 20% earns max (5 pts); bottom 40% earns 0 pts.

The score is not an absolute indicator. High-score stocks can still decline, and low-score stocks can rise. Always combine with other information and use your own judgment.

Win Probability Calculation

A heuristic estimate combining score, pattern confidence, and RS rank with custom weights. It is derived from statistical analysis of past backtests — use it as a reference, not a guarantee.

probability ≈ (score × 0.5 + pattern_confidence × 0.3 + rs_rank × 0.2) × adjustment_factor

Score vs Probability

Score and probability are both important but measure different things. Use each appropriately.

MetricWhat It MeasuresBest Used When
Score (0–100) Overall technical environment quality. 5-axis evaluation: Trend, Pattern, Momentum, Volume, RS. Checking if the stock is technically in good shape right now
Probability (0–100%) Estimated win probability blending historical pattern win rates with score. Assessing whether the expected value of the entry is sufficient

Recommended Priority Order

1
RR ratio firstEven the best signal can have negative expected value if RR is below 1.5. Aim for RR ≥ 2.0.
2
Score ≥ 50 as baselineHigher score = more indicators aligned simultaneously. Score ≥ 50 tends to show statistically meaningful edge.
3
Probability ≥ 60% for confirmationProbability depends heavily on pattern type and strength. Signals ≥ 60% tend toward higher backtested win rates.

💡 When in doubt, prioritize score. Probability is heavily influenced by score, so high score usually means high probability too. Score ≥ 60, Prob ≥ 60%, RR ≥ 2.0 — the ideal “triple confluence” setup.

Chart Pattern Reference

Currently detects 31+ patterns. Multiple pattern confluence increases reliability.

Reversal Patterns

Inverse H&S
Reversal

One of the most reliable reversal patterns. Three lows (left shoulder, head, right shoulder). Confirmed entry on neckline breakout with rising volume. Target = head-to-neckline distance projected upward.

Double Bottom
Reversal

After testing nearly the same low twice, price breaks above the intermediate high (neckline). Reliability increases when the second bottom bounces to the same level as the first.

Triple Bottom
Reversal

Extension of Double Bottom. Three tests of the same support level before a breakout. Takes longer to form — more reliable but rarer than Double Bottom.

Falling Wedge
Reversal

A converging range where both highs and lows decline. A strong reversal signal in downtrends. Ideal when volume increases on the upper trendline breakout. Opposite of a rising wedge — this is a bullish pattern.

Continuation Patterns

Cup with Handle
Continuation

A rounded base (cup) followed by a small consolidation (handle) before the breakout. Forms during uptrends and is considered evidence of institutional accumulation. Volume contraction in the handle is ideal.

Bull Flag
Continuation

A tight downward drift (flag) after a sharp surge (pole), followed by an upside breakout. Volume contracts during the flag and surges on the break. Target = the pole’s length projected from the breakout.

VCP (Volatility Contraction)
Continuation

A series of progressively smaller price contractions (e.g. 15%→10%→7%→4%). Volatility contraction signals energy buildup. Mark Minervini’s growth-stock technique.

50MA Pullback
Continuation

A pullback to the 50-day moving average during an uptrend, followed by a bounce. A healthy consolidation in an uptrend that often provides the entry point for the next leg higher.

Channel Pullback
Continuation

A pullback to the lower trendline (support) of an ascending channel, followed by a bounce. More reliable when the channel is well-defined. Ideal entry: volume contracts at the lower touch and surges on the bounce.

Breakout Patterns

Resistance Breakout
Breakout

Price breaks above a resistance level that has been tested multiple times, accompanied by rising volume. The more tests, the stronger the breakout tends to be.

New 52-Week High
Breakout

Price makes a new 52-week high. A strong momentum signal breaking through long-term resistance. At all-time highs there is no overhead resistance, so further gains are expected.

BB Squeeze (Bollinger)
Breakout

A breakout following Bollinger Band contraction (squeeze). An upside break from band-width below the 20th percentile signals impending volatility expansion.

Ascending Triangle
Breakout

Price is capped by a horizontal resistance while lows rise. Gradually increasing buying pressure eventually breaks above the resistance.

Symmetric Triangle
Breakout

A converging triangle where highs decline and lows rise symmetrically. Direction is uncertain — wait to confirm the breakout. An upside break is a bullish signal.

Donchian Break
Breakout

Price breaks above the N-day high (upper Donchian channel). Popularized by the Turtle Traders. A simple, mechanically detectable price-range breakout.

SR Flip
Breakout

After breaking above a prior resistance, that price zone flips to support. A bounce from the new support confirms a reliable continuation signal.

Moving Average Patterns

Golden Cross (Short-term)
Moving Avg

SMA5 crosses above SMA25. Short-term trend reversal signal. Highly sensitive but prone to false signals — use in combination with other indicators.

Golden Cross (Medium-term)
Moving Avg

SMA25 crosses above SMA75. Medium-term trend reversal signal. Lags the short-term GC but is more reliable.

Golden Cross (Long-term)
Moving Avg

SMA75 crosses above SMA200. Signals a long-term bear-to-bull transition. Low frequency but high significance.

Perfect Order
Moving Avg

SMA5 > SMA25 > SMA75 > SMA200 with all MAs pointing upward. The strongest uptrend signal. Tends to generate high scores — beware of entering near a top.

MA Compression
Moving Avg

Multiple moving averages converging toward a single point. Evidence of energy buildup — a pre-breakout setup. Direction must be confirmed.

Ichimoku Patterns

Cloud Break (Kumo Break)
Ichimoku

Price breaks above the Ichimoku cloud (resistance zone). Thinner clouds are easier to break; after the break, the cloud becomes support. Widely watched by institutional traders.

Ichimoku Bullish Triple
Ichimoku

All three conditions true simultaneously: ① Price above cloud ② Tenkan > Kijun ③ Chikou above past price. The strongest Ichimoku bullish signal. Rare because all conditions must align.

Divergence Patterns

RSI Divergence
Divergence

“Bullish divergence” where price makes lower lows but RSI makes higher lows. Signals weakening selling pressure — often precedes a bottom reversal.

MACD Divergence
Divergence

Price and MACD histogram diverge in direction — a sign of potential trend reversal. More reliable when occurring simultaneously with RSI divergence.

RSI Hidden Divergence
Divergence

“Hidden divergence” where price makes higher highs but RSI makes lower highs. Unlike regular divergence, this signals trend continuation. Suggests re-acceleration after a pullback.

OBV Divergence
Divergence

Price falling or flat while OBV (cumulative volume) rises. Evidence of quiet institutional accumulation. OBV improvement often leads price recovery.

Candlestick Patterns

Hammer
Candlestick

A candle with a long lower shadow and short body. At the end of a downtrend, it’s a strong bullish reversal signal. Detected when lower shadow is 2× or more the body length.

Bullish Engulfing
Candlestick

A large bullish candle completely engulfs the previous bearish candle. Strong evidence of buying pressure. More reliable with rising volume.

Morning Star
Candlestick

Three-candle pattern: bearish candle → small gap-down candle (or doji) → large bullish candle. A strong reversal signal from downtrend to uptrend.

Three White Soldiers
Candlestick

Three consecutive bullish candles, each opening above the prior close. Signals sustained buying pressure. Appearing after a sharp rally may indicate overheating.

Piercing Line
Candlestick

After a large bearish candle, price gaps down but recovers above the midpoint of the prior candle. A two-candle bottom-reversal signal showing selling pressure dissipating rapidly.

Three Gap Reversal
Candlestick

A reversal signal after three consecutive downside gaps. Known in classic trading lore as a bottom signal following panic-driven selling.

Wyckoff Accumulation

Wyckoff Spring
Wyckoff

Detects the Wyckoff accumulation phase: Selling Climax → volume dry-up (Spring) → Sign of Strength. A breakout after institutional accumulation at the bottom. One of the most reliable long-term signals.

Target & Stop Loss

Target Price Calculation (priority order)

Candidates are evaluated in priority order; the first candidate exceeding the current price by +2% (minimum margin) is adopted.

PriorityMethodFormula
1stPattern targetPattern height added to breakout point (e.g. Double Bottom depth ÷ neckline × upward direction)
2ndFibonacci 127%Recent swing range × 1.272 added to the low
3rdFibonacci 162%Recent swing range × 1.618 added to the low
4th (fallback)ATR × 3Current price + ATR(14d) × 3 (always above current price)

When a past pattern target is below the current price (stock already surged past target), the system automatically falls back to Fibonacci or ATR multiples.

Stop-Loss Price

Stop Loss = Current Price − ATR(14d) × 2

Capped so that stop-loss is always at least −1% below current price (upper bound: current price × 0.99).

RR Ratio (Risk-Reward)

RR = (Target − Current Price) ÷ (Current Price − Stop Loss)

Backtest Limitations

  • Data accumulation needed: Only signals since scanner launch are evaluated. 100+ trades needed for statistical significance (1–3 months).
  • Slippage & liquidity not modeled: Real fills may differ from theoretical prices.
  • Taxes & commissions not included: Actual returns will be lower.
  • No simultaneous entry optimization: All signals evaluated independently.

⚠️ Good past backtest results do not guarantee future performance. Market regime changes can cause the same signals to produce very different outcomes.

Entry Decision Guide

The scan runs on the prior day’s closing prices. Between reviewing results in the evening and entering the next morning, prices will have moved. Do not use scan results directly — reassess at the open.

After-Market to Open Flow

1
After Market (4 PM ET onwards): Review scan results Check score, pattern, RR ratio. Narrow your watch list and set tentative limit prices based on current closing prices.
2
Pre-Market (9:15–9:29 AM ET): Check pre-market prices Use your brokerage app to check pre-market quotes. If gap-up exceeds 2%, recalculate RR before the open.
3
Market Open (9:30 AM ET): Final decision at the open Enter the actual open price in the order memo’s “Entry Price” field and recheck RR. RR ≥ 1.5 → enter. RR < 1.5 → skip.
4
Post-Entry: Register in Position Manager Enter your actual fill price and share count. Target, stop-loss, and ATR will be auto-filled.

Gap-Up Entry Judgment

Stocks in strong uptrends or with positive catalysts may open above the prior close (gap up). A gap-up itself can signal strength, but always recheck the RR ratio.

RR Recalculation

Target and stop-loss prices are based on technical levels (resistance, ATR, etc.) and do not change with the entry price. Only the RR ratio changes.

Example: RR Deterioration from Gap-Up
At Scan CloseAfter Gap-Up
Entry Price$100.00$106.00 (+6%)
Target$133.00$133.00 (unchanged)
Stop Loss$90.00$90.00 (unchanged)
RR Ratio3.3:11.7:1

Enter the open price in the order memo’s “Entry Price” field to automatically see updated RR and entry eligibility.

Gap Size vs Action

Gap vs Prior CloseActionReason
+1–2% Within expectation. Verify RR then enter. Minor gap with small RR impact.
+2–5% Recalculate RR. Enter if ≥ 1.5. Visible RR deterioration — confirmation required.
+5%+ Generally skip. Wait for a pullback. RR almost certainly fails. High risk of buying the top.

The “Wait for Pullback” Option

Gap-up stocks sometimes pull back to the prior day’s high around 10–11 AM ET. For breakout patterns where the broken level becomes new support (Resistance Breakout, SR Flip), this retest can offer a better entry price than the open.

When pullback-waiting works
Patterns where the broken price level becomes new support: Resistance Breakout, New 52W High, SR Flip. The breakout level retested is the second entry point.
When pullback-waiting is risky
If volume is thin on the breakout, the “pullback” may become a full reversal (false break). Only use this approach for breakouts with volume ratio ≥ 1.5×.

Scaling In

Instead of entering your full position at once, build it in multiple tranches. Effective when confidence, liquidity, or market conditions are not 100% ideal.

Core idea: “Limit damage if wrong.” Enter half first, observe the market’s reaction, then add the rest. If wrong, only the first tranche takes the loss.

Scaling-In Pitfalls

⚠ What NOT to Do

  • Add when price drops (averaging down) — Scaling in means adding after confirmation of upward movement, not adding into losses.
  • Keep the same stop-loss when adding shares — More shares = more risk. Recalculate stop and share count before adding.
  • Exceed your risk budget because tranche 1 is profitable — Total risk (loss if stop is hit) must stay within 1–2% of capital.

✓ When Full-Size Entry Is Appropriate

  • Score ≥ 80, Probability ≥ 65% — strong signal
  • Sufficient liquidity (daily volume ≥ $1M/day)
  • Breakout clearly confirmed with volume surge (≥ 2×)
  • RR ratio ≥ 2.0
  • Market environment is bullish

If a ★ Recommended badge appears (Score ≥ 60, Prob ≥ 60%, RR ≥ 2.0) and no 💧 liquidity warning is shown, a full-size entry may be appropriate.

Risk Management Principle: Whether scaling in or entering full-size, total risk per trade must stay within 1–2% of capital. Scaling in is not about distributing the stop-loss — it’s about incrementally building risk as confidence grows. Set your stop-loss and share count from tranche 1, and manage subsequent tranches within the same risk envelope.
